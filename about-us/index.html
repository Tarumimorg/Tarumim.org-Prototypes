About us

Tarumim is a nonprofit environmental organisation founded by a small group of activists in October 2018. We are exploring how to facilitate sustainable livelihoods and socially resilient networks, empowering grassroots reforestation initiatives and alternative economic models.

Our base is in London, UK, and our initial operations are in Minas Gerais, Brazil, though we hope to expand beyond the state and indeed the country. Learn more


Background


In 2008, one of the founders of Tarumim swam in a beautiful stream near São Sebastião da Boa Vista, a tiny village in the Valley of Jequitinhonha. On a second visit in 2015, the stream had completely dried up. The dry season grows longer each year, and stories of droughts and floods are becoming more commonplace around the world as water cycles are disrupted.


Minas Gerais has lost over 90% of its Atlantic forest in the last three centuries, with devastating effects on the water cycle, soil health, biodiversity and food security. Sinking more wells is a short term solution to water shortages but it depletes the water table, and if the water drawn is not used to regenerate the land it can cause further problems. Also related to deforestation is the more global problem articulated by the recent IPCC report noting that we have a mere 12-year window before climate change becomes irreversible. Resolving these problems requires a fundamental shift in terms of how we relate to the world around us. If we are to make any more than marginal impacts, we need to address our abusive relationship with the land and find ways to live in synergy with the plant kingdom and the other animals that depend on it.


The problem is daunting and complex. The solution, at least, is simple.


Reforestation


Reforestation causes significant local increases in both rainfall and the health of the water table, while also being an effective method of carbon capture. To make a difference to global warming, however, it needs to be done on a massive scale.


Tarumim is exploring how people outside of Brazil can help small farmers regenerate forests in ways that are sustainable not just environmentally but economically in what is one of the poorer parts of the country. Our work begins with a handful of projects employing Syntropic Agroforestry to increase tree cover and protect the soil from continued degradation while improving water flow. The extremely high yields of these farms plots means that they can support small farmers, improve local economic activity, and encourage similar experiments by other land-owners. The techniques are tried and tested in Brazil, and we will be employing Brazilian teachers to introduce them to rural regions.


People who want to make an impact in these areas can help fund regeneration initiatives geared towards food security and resilience in regions already facing the effects of climate change. Our Brazilian team will incentivise land owners with free agroforestry workshops in rural towns, as well as providing material assistance and support to agriculturalists who wish to reforest their land.


Along with researchers from Brazilian universities we will be monitoring projects, measuring ecological, social and economic impacts, and reporting back to partners in our networks and beyond. Our goals are to maximise impacts, to be visible in the media and the marketplace, and to catalyse the return of trees and abundant life to one of the most exploited regions of the planet. To that end, we have put together a core team of a PhD soil ecologist, a start-up online entrepreneur and a writer with extensive experience living in Brazil and making connections.


Nurturing abundance


Syntropic Agroforestry is a holistic way of regenerating the land, increasing tree cover and biodiversity while enriching the soil, capturing carbon and bringing water sources such as springs and rivers back to life. High agricultural yields of up to 40 tonnes per hectare make the farms among the trees highly competitive for small farmers whose other options may be limited to pasture or monoculture. These permaculture methods are integrated and self-sustaining, requiring neither fertiliser nor expensive machinery. We envision that with modest initial investments associates can soon become self-sufficient and able to contribute to an expanding network of practitioners moving seeds and ideas between projects and communities. Neighbours will walk among the growing trees and see the springs and rivers returning to life, and also want to benefit from the abundance that sustainable practices can bring forth from the land.


Conventional agriculture is fragile to the harsh weather associated with climate change, and this is already obvious to land-workers in Minas Gerais. Agroforestry plots, on the other hand, can thrive even in difficult conditions. We foresee that sustainable land practices will be widely adopted as the future unfolds.


First seeds


Our first project is beginning in Boa Vista, and we are in conversation with Brazilians regarding another five initiatives.


Loose targets:

By February 2019: Attract enough funding to run 10 pilots.

By December 2020: Deliver workshops to 1000s of land-workers, and be assisting 100 new agroforestry experiments.

By December 2023: Measure the productivity of our associates in kilotonnes, and the number of trees planted in the hundreds of thousands.
