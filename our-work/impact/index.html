Impact

Syntropic Agriculture is a young movement and the statistics it produces are impressive, but the number of studies is limited. Our pilot projects are designed to test the ecological and socio-economic impacts of projects using sustainable land-use practices in different environments. Working alongside researchers from Brazilian institutions, we will be analysing soil samples and aerial imagery as well as the commercial health of our associates, and monitoring the impact of workshops on the farming practices of attendees.


Local ecology


Increasing evidence shows a link between tree cover and rainfall patterns, and Syntropic Agriculture projects have been spectacularly successful. In one plot of 480 hectares that had been logged dry, the forest has come back and fourteen springs have returned to life. Seventeen streams now run all year, insects are thriving and the surface temperature has dropped in the microregion. Even rainfall has increased.


Global environment


A hectare of agroforest managed according to the principles of Syntropic Agriculture may capture 10, then 20 then 40 tonnes of carbon per year over the first 15 years of growth. In terms of reducing and one day reversing climate change, reforestation is key. Forests are also much more resilient to the effects of climate change.


Economic


Along with 40 tonnes of carbon absorbed and stored in the soil and branches, 40 tonnes of organic, high-quality, diverse agricultural produce is generated for sustenance and sale. One farm in Bahia sells high-quality cacao to an Italian chocolate manufacturer at four times normal price.


Tarumim will also be mapping out and focusing on areas of ecological significance where impacts will be greatest, such as degraded rivers, biodiversity hotspots and marginal zones on the edge of native forest.
