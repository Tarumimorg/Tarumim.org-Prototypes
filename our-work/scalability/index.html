Project development

Brazil has a wide variety of environments, and each presents different challenges and opportunities. We encourage individuals and associations to approach us with an idea of the assistance they need to get a reforestation project going, so we can discuss with them how we can best support them in the beginning stages. Different situations will lend themselves to different approaches, and different teachers will use different techniques. By monitoring case studies in a diverse portfolio of crowdsourced models for land regeneration, we can learn about what works where and how to maximise impacts.


Projects in development


Pasture and Stream regeneration, Boa Vista


Small-scale agriculture is the way of life for many people in Boa Vista. Reforesting doesn’t make economic sense according to the conventional paradigm of agriculture, and under economic pressures rural people are rarely in a position to experiment with radical and unfamiliar practices. Tarumim has already assisted by sinking a well, and will provide further support including hosting agroforestry workshops in the village. The land-owners can reforest and regenerate their stream and their springs at the same time as generating valuable produce, showcasing abundant and sustainable techniques of land management to other people of the village.


Santa Luzia Network


Santa Luzia in Minas Gerais is a beautiful colonial town with windy roads and small farms dotted around. Claudio wants to drive the cows off his pasture and return it to the forest. Thiago has a little land and a business building sustainable houses from bamboo, and he wants to incorporate food security and agroforestry into his designs. Both have expressed interest in hosting a workshop, and have friends also keen to attend. Informal networks like this mean that a small initial investment for a workshop can potentially stimulate several experiments and lead to many hectares of forest cover.


Quebrada Do Índio


Philippe’s land is part native forest, with a beautiful waterfall that day-trippers swim in.


Part of his land is pasture, which he has been reforesting while also raising endangered Rhea birds for release into the wild. He is limited both in disposable income and time, as he works in the capital of the state and can only visit on weekends, and he is also resisting the State Grid Corporation of China, who are seeking to cut down ancient forest to save a few hundred meters of electric wires.


An investment and perhaps some volunteer labour would allow him to give some land over to Agroforestry. The income generated would easily be sufficient to house a few enthusiastic stewards on the land, and any profits will be ploughed back into ecosystem regeneration.


School Gardens, Eco-Villages and Other Initiatives


We are in conversation with other projects about entering into partnerships. One project is an eco-village where they are expanding their Agroforestry operations. Another combines ecology with alternative education, setting up permaculture projects in partnership with rural schools in parts of the state where slash and burn agriculture is still generally seen as the only option. The impacts of educating both children and their parents about harmonious ways to engage with nature are incalculable.


We know a lot of cool people in Minas Gerais, and we would be very happy to hear from more.
