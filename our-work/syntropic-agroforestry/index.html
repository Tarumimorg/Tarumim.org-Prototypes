Syntropic Agroforestry

Ecosystem and Economic Health through Reforestation


Poverty, population growth and intensive farming have put immense pressures on the land. While people may want to conserve their forests or regenerate pasture, they are often too poor to have that luxury.


With Syntropic Agroforestry, land is regenerated by planting grass, herb and tree species in sequence, working in synch with natural cycles to provide food security and local self-sufficiency. ‘Syntropy’ refers to ‘increasing complexity’. Planting is mixed, not monoculture, and it generates agricultural produce from the very first months to sustain the livelihoods of those living in synergy with nature.


One hectare produces:


Agroforestry:

- 40 tonnes of up to 50 different crops over an extended period

Monoculture:

- Around 3 tonnes of soya (national average), all at once.

Cattle:

- Just over one cow (1.08 cows, national average).


High Yield, High Intensity


Trees are heavily pruned as they grow, which stimulates intense growth while allowing light to the crops. The cuttings protect the soil and enrich it as they biodegrade, preparing the land for larger species and the next stage of regeneration. Agroforestry sites have on average

4kg of dry organic matter per m², producing  6-10 kg of mulch per year. The mulch protects the soil from erosion and preserves water. Crops require no fertiliser, and can go for over a month without watering.


Ecological Solutions must address Human Problems to be part of a Sustainable Future.


Converting pasture and monoculture into productive forest is a different strategy to most other reforestation or conservation initiatives. Tarumim supports and researches high-biodiversity, high-yield, high-impact systems of sustainable agroforestry as attractive alternatives to conventional farming for rural people.


An integrated system of land regeneration can provide a sustainable income and housing for stewards of the land, while mitigating the consequences of climate change at the local and global level.


As coffee farmer João comments:


“This system enabled us to reconcile all our desires. The regeneration of the forest takes place in a highly productive fashion, improving soil, water, and food production, while integrating we human beings into the natural system, and serving as a vehicle for community integration.”
